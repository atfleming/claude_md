# Storytelling — StoryBrand framework

When writing or reviewing website copy, structure messaging around the StoryBrand 7-part framework. The customer is the hero; the brand is the guide. Keep language so simple a third-grader could understand it — never make the customer "burn calories" to understand what we do.

## 1. Character (what the customer wants)
- Define the ONE thing the customer wants (not many things).
- If there are multiple offerings, find the umbrella desire that covers them.
- State it in plain language, no insider jargon.

## 2. Problem (external, internal, philosophical)
- External: the tangible obstacle in their way.
- Internal: how that obstacle makes them feel (frustrated, confused, alone).
- Philosophical: why it's "just plain wrong" they have to deal with this.
- Keep the three connected; don't list too many problems.

## 3. Guide (empathy + authority)
- Empathy: show we understand their struggle (keep it brief, genuine).
- Authority: prove we can help (testimonials, success stories, credentials relevant to THEIR problem).

## 4. Plan (the path forward)
- Give a simple 3–4 step plan. Never more than 4 steps.
- Use short, clear step titles. No insider language, no feature-dumping.
- Choose pre-purchase ("how to engage us") or post-purchase ("what happens after they buy") as fits the page.

## 5. Call to Action
- Direct CTA: the clear ask (e.g., "Schedule a Call," "Buy Now").
- Transitional CTA: a low-risk offer for those not ready to buy (free guide, webinar, quiz, consultation) — ideally in exchange for an email.
- For CTA copy, answer: What's their motivation to click? What do they get?

## 6. Stakes / Failure (what's at risk)
- Name the cost of inaction. What do they lose if nothing changes?
- On a page, agitate the problem BEFORE presenting the happy outcome.

## 7. Success (imagine the win)
- Paint a vivid picture of life after the problem is solved.
- Show both failure and success on the page to motivate action.

## Guiding principles
- Hero = customer, never the brand.
- Clarity over cleverness; simplicity wins.
- Pare every section down to its essential, single idea.
- On-page order in practice: want → problem → guide → plan → CTA → stakes → success.
